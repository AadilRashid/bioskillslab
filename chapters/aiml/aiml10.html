The Problem With Ordinary Autoencoders

Run this chapter in Google Colab (GPU recommended)

The autoencoder from Chapter 9 learned to compress 20,000 gene expression values into a 64-dimensional latent vector. When you visualised that latent space, cancer types clustered together. That is useful. But the latent space has a fundamental limitation: it is not continuous or structured.

Points in the latent space that correspond to training samples are meaningful — they represent real gene expression profiles. But the space between those points is largely empty and unpredictable. If you pick a point halfway between a breast cancer sample and a lung cancer sample in latent space and decode it, the reconstruction may be biologically nonsensical rather than an interpolation between the two tumour types.

This matters for three biological applications: patient stratification (discovering a continuous spectrum of disease states), data generation (simulating new biological samples for analysis), and understanding the structure of biological variation. All three require a latent space that is smooth, continuous, and structured. Variational autoencoders provide exactly that.

From Points to Distributions

The key insight of a variational autoencoder (VAE) is simple but profound. Instead of encoding each sample as a single point in latent space, encode it as a probability distribution — specifically, a Gaussian distribution defined by a mean and a variance.

Standard autoencoder:
  Input → Encoder → Point z → Decoder → Reconstruction

Variational autoencoder:
  Input → Encoder → Mean (mu) + Variance (log_var) → Sample z ~ N(mu, var) → Decoder → Reconstruction

During training, instead of always using the exact encoded point, you sample from the distribution. This forces the decoder to learn to reconstruct from a region of latent space, not just a single point. The result is a latent space where nearby points decode to similar outputs — the smoothness you need for meaningful interpolation.

The VAE Loss Function

The VAE is trained with two objectives simultaneously, which is what makes it different from a standard autoencoder.

The first objective is reconstruction: the decoded output should resemble the input, just as in a regular autoencoder. This is measured by reconstruction loss, typically mean squared error for continuous data.

The second objective is regularisation of the latent space: the learned distributions should stay close to a standard normal distribution (mean 0, variance 1). This prevents the encoder from spreading different samples arbitrarily far apart in latent space and ensures the space is dense and continuous. This regularisation is measured by KL divergence.

VAE loss = Reconstruction loss + Beta * KL divergence

Reconstruction loss:  how well the decoder reproduces the input
KL divergence:        how close the encoded distributions are to N(0,1)
Beta:                 a hyperparameter controlling the balance

The KL divergence term is what gives the VAE its structure. It acts like a pressure forcing all the encoded distributions toward the centre of latent space while the reconstruction loss pulls them apart to distinguish different samples. The equilibrium between these two forces produces a smooth, organised latent space.

The Reparameterisation Trick

There is a technical challenge in training VAEs: sampling from a distribution is not differentiable, which means you cannot backpropagate gradients through the sampling step. The solution is the reparameterisation trick — rather than sampling z directly from the distribution, express z as a deterministic function of the parameters plus a random noise term:

z = mu + epsilon * std      where epsilon ~ N(0, 1)

Instead of:   z ~ N(mu, sigma^2)     [not differentiable]
We use:       z = mu + sigma * eps   [differentiable with respect to mu and sigma]

This moves the randomness into a separate noise variable that the network does not need to differentiate through. Gradients flow through mu and sigma, allowing the encoder parameters to be optimised normally.

Building a VAE in PyTorch

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, latent_dim):
        super().__init__()

        # Encoder: input -> hidden -> (mu, log_var)
        self.encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim // 2),
            nn.ReLU()
        )
        self.fc_mu      = nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, latent_dim)
        self.fc_log_var = nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, latent_dim)

        # Decoder: latent -> hidden -> output
        self.decoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(latent_dim, hidden_dim // 2),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, input_dim)
        )

    def encode(self, x):
        h       = self.encoder(x)
        mu      = self.fc_mu(h)
        log_var = self.fc_log_var(h)
        return mu, log_var

    def reparameterise(self, mu, log_var):
        std     = torch.exp(0.5 * log_var)   # convert log_var to std
        epsilon = torch.randn_like(std)        # sample noise
        return mu + epsilon * std             # reparameterisation trick

    def decode(self, z):
        return self.decoder(z)

    def forward(self, x):
        mu, log_var = self.encode(x)
        z           = self.reparameterise(mu, log_var)
        x_recon     = self.decode(z)
        return x_recon, mu, log_var

def vae_loss(x_recon, x, mu, log_var, beta=1.0):
    recon_loss = F.mse_loss(x_recon, x, reduction='sum')
    # KL divergence: -0.5 * sum(1 + log_var - mu^2 - exp(log_var))
    kl_loss    = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + log_var - mu.pow(2) - log_var.exp())
    return recon_loss + beta * kl_loss

Training the VAE

vae = VAE(input_dim=X_train_scaled.shape[1],
          hidden_dim=512,
          latent_dim=32)
vae_opt = torch.optim.Adam(vae.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

X_train_t = torch.FloatTensor(X_train_scaled)
X_test_t  = torch.FloatTensor(X_test_scaled)
loader    = DataLoader(TensorDataset(X_train_t),
                       batch_size=256, shuffle=True)

for epoch in range(100):
    vae.train()
    total_loss = 0
    for (x_batch,) in loader:
        vae_opt.zero_grad()
        x_recon, mu, log_var = vae(x_batch)
        loss = vae_loss(x_recon, x_batch, mu, log_var, beta=1.0)
        loss.backward()
        vae_opt.step()
        total_loss += loss.item()
    if (epoch + 1) % 20 == 0:
        print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}: loss = {total_loss/len(loader):.1f}")

Exploring the Latent Space

Once trained, the VAE latent space has properties that the standard autoencoder lacks. You can explore it in several biologically interesting ways.

Visualising the Structured Latent Space

vae.eval()
with torch.no_grad():

    mu_test,  _ = vae.encode(X_test_t)
    Z_train_vae = mu_train.numpy()
    Z_test_vae  = mu_test.numpy()

The VAE latent space is typically smoother and more continuous than a standard autoencoder. Cancer types still cluster together, but the transitions between clusters are gradual rather than abrupt. This reflects the biological reality that cancer is not a set of perfectly discrete categories but a continuous spectrum of molecular states.

Patient Stratification — This Is Where Your Research Lives

Within a single cancer type, the VAE latent space reveals further structure. Breast cancer, for example, is not one disease — it has at least four molecular subtypes (Luminal A, Luminal B, HER2-enriched, Basal-like) with different prognoses and treatment responses. The VAE can discover this substructure without being told it exists.

# Focus on breast cancer samples only

plt.ylabel('Latent PC2')

If the VAE learned meaningful representations, the four PAM50 subtypes should separate in the BRCA latent subspace. This is unsupervised discovery of clinically meaningful disease subgroups — the model was never told about PAM50, yet its latent space recovers it. This is the kind of finding that makes VAEs valuable in cancer biology.

The Beta-VAE and Disentanglement

The beta hyperparameter in the VAE loss controls the balance between reconstruction quality and latent space regularity. Setting beta greater than 1 increases the pressure toward N(0,1), producing a more structured but less detailed latent space. This is called a Beta-VAE.

Higher beta values encourage disentanglement — a property where different dimensions of the latent space independently control different aspects of the data. In genomics, a fully disentangled VAE might have one dimension controlling overall tumour grade, another controlling tissue of origin, and another controlling immune infiltration — completely independently. Disentanglement is an active research area, and scVI (the next chapter) is partly motivated by applying these ideas to single-cell data.

Reality Check
  • Does the VAE latent space show more structure than the standard autoencoder? If not, the additional complexity may not be warranted.
  • Does the reconstruction loss converge? A VAE that does not reconstruct well has not learned useful representations.
  • Do clinically meaningful subgroups emerge in the latent space without supervision? If yes, the VAE found real biology.
  • Is the beta hyperparameter tuned appropriately? Too high and reconstruction fails; too low and the latent space loses its structure.
