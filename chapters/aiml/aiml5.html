Chapter 5: Supervised vs Unsupervised Learning

~40 min Beginner Supervised, Unsupervised, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, PCA, k-means

The Biological Question

Two scenarios that look similar but require completely different approaches:

Scenario A: You have gene expression profiles from 500 tumour samples, each labelled with its cancer subtype from pathology reports. You want to build a model that predicts cancer subtype for new samples.

Scenario B: You have single-cell RNA-seq data from 20,000 cells. You have no labels. You want to discover what cell types are present.

Scenario A is supervised learning. Scenario B is unsupervised learning. The distinction determines which algorithms you can use, how you evaluate results, and what biological claims you can make.

Supervised Learning — Learning From Labels

In supervised learning, every training example has a known label. The algorithm learns a mapping from features to labels — a function that can then be applied to new, unlabelled samples.

Supervised learning workflow:
  
  Labelled data (features + known answers)
          ↓
  Train model (learn the mapping)
          ↓
  Evaluate on test set (honest performance estimate)
          ↓
  Apply to new samples (predict labels)

The labels are your supervision — they tell the algorithm what correct looks like. Without labels, supervised learning is impossible.

Where Labels Come From in Biology

Labels come from experiments, clinical records, or expert annotation. Some examples:

  • Cancer subtype from pathology reports or molecular profiling panels
  • Tumour vs normal from surgical annotation
  • Cell type from fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) with antibody markers
  • Regulatory element vs non-regulatory from ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq experiments
  • Pathogenic variant from ClinVar or functional assays
  • Drug responder vs non-responder from clinical trial outcomes

The quality of labels is a ceiling on model quality. If two pathologists disagree on cancer subtype for 20% of cases, a model trained on those labels cannot reliably exceed 80% accuracy — not because of algorithmic limitations, but because the labels themselves are inconsistent.

Key Supervised Algorithms — A Preview

You will build models with all of these in Chapters 7-10. Here is the conceptual introduction:

Logistic Regression finds a linear boundary in feature space that separates classes. Despite the name, it is a classification algorithm. It is interpretable — you can look at the coefficients and understand which features drive predictions. It often performs surprisingly well even on complex genomics data, and should always be your baseline.

Random Forest builds hundreds of decision trees, each trained on a random subset of features and samples, then averages their predictions. The randomness makes it robust to overfitting and noise. It handles thousands of features (genes) well, provides feature importance scores, and works even when features outnumber samples — a common situation in genomics.

Neural Networks learn hierarchical representations of data through layers of weighted connections. They excel when the signal is complex and non-linear, and when you have enough data to support the parameter count. In genomics, they become necessary when the relationships between features are too complex for random forests to capture — for example, learning spatial patterns in sequence data.

Unsupervised Learning — Discovering Structure Without Labels

Unsupervised learning finds structure in data without being told what to look for. There are no labels. The algorithm discovers patterns based purely on the similarities and differences between samples.

Unsupervised learning workflow:
  
  Unlabelled data (features only, no answers)
          ↓
  Find structure (clusters, components, embeddings)
          ↓
  Interpret results (what do the patterns mean biologically?)
          ↓
  Validate against known biology (are these real?)

The critical difference from supervised learning is that the last step — interpretation and validation — is entirely on you. The algorithm has found mathematical structure. Whether that structure corresponds to real biology requires domain knowledge.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

You covered PCA in the Statistics for Biology course. Here it appears as an unsupervised learning method. PCA finds the directions of maximum variance in high-dimensional data and projects everything onto those directions.

In genomics, PCA is used to:

  • Visualise 20,000-dimensional gene expression data in 2D
  • Identify batch effects (samples from different labs often cluster by batch in PCA)
  • Reduce dimensions before clustering or supervised classification
  • Find the major axes of variation in a patient cohort
PCA is often the right answer

Before reaching for a deep learning model, always run PCA. If your samples separate clearly by the label of interest in PCA space, a simple classifier will work well. If they do not separate, even a complex model will struggle. PCA tells you how easy the problem is before you invest compute time in complex methods.

k-Means Clustering

k-means is the simplest clustering algorithm. You specify k (the number of clusters) and the algorithm assigns each sample to the nearest cluster centre, then updates cluster centres, and repeats until stable.

k-means algorithm:
  1. Choose k (number of clusters)
  2. Randomly initialise k cluster centres
  3. Assign each sample to nearest centre
  4. Update each centre to the mean of its assigned samples
  5. Repeat steps 3-4 until assignments stop changing

The main weakness of k-means is that you must specify k in advance, and the result depends on the random initialisation. Run it multiple times and take the best result. For single-cell analysis, more sophisticated algorithms like Leiden (which we use in the single-cell chapters) are preferred.

UMAP — Nonlinear Dimensionality Reduction

UMAP is not strictly a clustering algorithm but it is almost always used alongside clustering in single-cell analysis. While PCA is linear, UMAP is nonlinear — it can preserve complex neighbourhood structures that PCA flattens out.

UMAP produces the signature "blobs on a dark background" visualisation you have seen in every single-cell paper. Each blob is a cluster of cells with similar expression profiles. UMAP preserves local structure very well but distorts global distances — two blobs being far apart in UMAP does not necessarily mean they are biologically very different.

UMAP is for visualisation, not analysis

Never perform statistical analysis on UMAP coordinates. The distances are not meaningful. UMAP is a visualisation tool. Perform your clustering on PCA components, then visualise the result with UMAP. The clusters shown in a UMAP plot should have been defined before UMAP was run.

Semi-Supervised Learning — The Middle Ground

In practice, many genomics problems fall between fully supervised and fully unsupervised. You have labels for some samples but not all. Semi-supervised learning uses the labelled samples to guide clustering of the unlabelled ones.

In single-cell biology, this is the standard approach: you have a few hundred cells with confirmed cell type labels from FACS sorting, and 50,000 cells without. You use the labelled cells to annotate the clusters found by unsupervised clustering. This is not fully supervised (you did not label 50,000 cells) and not fully unsupervised (you are using prior knowledge to interpret clusters).

Self-Supervised Learning — The Foundation Model Paradigm

Self-supervised learning is a third paradigm that has become central to modern biological AI. The model creates its own supervision from the data structure, without human labels.

The most common form: mask part of the input and train the model to predict what was masked. This is how BERT works for language, and how DNABERT and Geneformer work for DNA and cells respectively. Geneformer was pretrained by masking gene tokens in a cell's expression profile and training the model to predict which genes were masked — using 30 million single cells as training data, with no human labels required.

Self-supervised pretraining is why foundation models are so powerful: they learn rich biological representations from enormous unlabelled datasets, then can be fine-tuned on small labelled datasets for specific tasks.

Choosing the Right Approach

Situation Approach Examples
You have labelled training data Supervised Cancer classification, variant pathogenicity
No labels, want to discover structure Unsupervised Single-cell clustering, patient stratification
Some labels, mostly unlabelled Semi-supervised Single-cell annotation with reference dataset
Enormous unlabelled dataset, want rich representations Self-supervised Geneformer, DNABERT, scGPT pretraining
Not sure which group a sample belongs to Start with PCA Always the first step
Reality Check
  • Before any model: can PCA reveal the structure you are looking for?
  • Are your labels trustworthy? Inconsistent labels produce unreliable models.
  • If you found clusters unsupervised, have you validated them against known biology?
  • Is self-supervised pretraining on a foundation model actually necessary, or would a random forest on PCA components work just as well?

Running Question
