Chapter 3: Classification, Regression and Clustering

~35 min Beginner Classification, Regression, Clustering, Supervised, Unsupervised, PCA

The Biological Question

Before choosing any algorithm, you need to answer one question: what kind of output do you want?

Three scenarios from real biology. A patient's tumour is either lung cancer, breast cancer, or colon cancer — which one? That is asking for a category. What is the predicted survival time of this patient in months? That is asking for a number. These 10,000 cells — do they naturally group into distinct populations? That is asking to discover unknown groups without knowing what to look for.

These three scenarios correspond to the three fundamental tasks in machine learning: classification, regression, and clustering. Understanding which task you are solving determines every subsequent decision — which algorithm to use, which metrics to evaluate, and what biological claims you can make from the result.

Classification — Predicting a Category

Classification is the task of assigning an input to one of a fixed set of categories. The categories are defined in advance and every prediction must be one of them.

Gene expression profile   →  Cancer subtype (lung / breast / colon)
Cell gene expression      →  Cell type (T cell / B cell / monocyte)
DNA sequence              →  Regulatory element (yes / no)
Patient genomic profile   →  Treatment responder (yes / no)
Variant                   →  Pathogenic / Benign / Uncertain

When the output has exactly two categories — tumour vs normal, pathogenic vs benign — it is called binary classification. When there are three or more categories — cancer subtype, cell type annotation with dozens of possible types — it is called multi-class classification.

The intuition behind classification is drawing a decision boundary. Imagine plotting each tumour sample in a space where each axis is a gene's expression level. Samples of the same cancer type tend to cluster together in that space. Classification draws a boundary — a line or curve or hyperplane — that separates the groups. New samples that fall on one side are classified as type A; the other side, type B.

In practice you have 20,000 genes — 20,000 dimensions — not 2. The decision boundary exists in a space you cannot visualise. But the concept is identical. The algorithm is finding a mathematical surface that separates the classes in high-dimensional feature space.

Classification algorithms you will use in this course
  • Logistic Regression — simple, interpretable, often surprisingly competitive (Chapter 8)
  • Random Forest — robust, handles many features well, provides feature importance (Chapter 8)
  • Neural Networks — powerful for complex non-linear patterns, needs more data (Chapter 9)
  • Geneformer / scGPT — foundation models pretrained on millions of cells (Chapter 12)

Regression — Predicting a Number

Regression is the task of predicting a continuous numerical value rather than a category. The output is not "this sample belongs to group A" but "this sample's value is 47.3."

Gene expression profile   →  Survival time (months)
DNA sequence features     →  Gene expression level
Genomic variants          →  Protein abundance
Cell state features       →  Pseudotime (developmental stage)
Multi-omics profile       →  Drug response score (IC50)

Survival time is not a category. It is a continuous value that can be 3.2 months, 47.8 months, or any number in between. Predicting it requires regression. This distinction matters because the algorithms are different, the evaluation metrics are different, and the way you interpret results is different.

A common mistake is to force a continuous outcome into categories — "low survival" vs "high survival" by splitting on the median. This discards information and reduces statistical power. The threshold you choose is arbitrary and different thresholds give different results. Use regression when your outcome is continuous.

The reverse mistake also happens: treating a categorical label as a number. Assigning 1 to lung cancer, 2 to breast cancer, 3 to colon cancer, then running a regression. This introduces a false ordering — the model will interpret colon cancer as being "twice as much" as lung cancer, which is biologically meaningless. Match the algorithm to the output type.

Clustering — Discovering Groups Without Labels

Classification and regression both require labels — you need to know the correct answer for your training examples. Clustering does not. It finds natural groupings in data purely based on similarity, without being told what the groups are or how many there should be.

10,000 single cells       →  8 cell type clusters (discovered, not predefined)
500 tumour samples        →  4 molecular subtypes (discovered, not known in advance)
1,000 patients            →  3 stratification groups (discovered from data)
Protein interaction data  →  Functional modules (discovered by connectivity)

Clustering is unsupervised — there are no labels. The algorithm finds structure based on similarity in the feature space. Two cells with similar expression profiles across 20,000 genes get assigned to the same cluster. Two patients with similar multi-omics profiles get stratified together.

This is what happens when you run UMAP on single-cell data and see distinct blobs of cells. Each blob is a cluster where the cells are more similar to each other than to cells in other blobs. The algorithm found that structure without being told anything about cell biology.

Clusters are not guaranteed to be biology

Clustering finds groups that are mathematically distinct in your feature space. Those groups may correspond to real biological cell types — or they may reflect technical variation, sequencing depth differences, batch effects from different sequencing runs, or the particular normalisation method you used. Always validate clusters against known marker genes before claiming biological discovery. The algorithm found a mathematical pattern. Whether it is biology is a question only domain knowledge can answer.

The Three Tasks Side by Side

Task Output type Labels needed? Biological example
Classification Category Yes Cancer subtype, cell type, pathogenic variant
Regression Continuous number Yes Survival time, expression level, drug IC50
Clustering Discovered groups No Cell populations, tumour subtypes, patient groups

Where Does PCA Fit?

You encountered PCA in the Statistics for Biology course. Here it reappears, but in a different role. PCA is not classification, regression, or clustering — it is dimensionality reduction. It compresses high-dimensional data into fewer dimensions while preserving as much variation as possible.

PCA is used as a preprocessing step before clustering or classification. In single-cell analysis, the pipeline is almost always the same regardless of which tool you use:

Raw expression matrix (cells × 20,000 genes)
        ↓
Normalise and log-transform
        ↓
Select highly variable genes (~2,000)
        ↓
PCA (reduce to ~50 principal components)
        ↓
Nearest-neighbour graph on PCA components
        ↓
Clustering (Leiden or Louvain algorithm)
        ↓
UMAP (visualise the clusters)

This exact pipeline runs millions of times every week in labs worldwide. Chapter 7 applies it to TCGA cancer data, and Chapter 11 applies it to single-cell PBMC data. Understanding that PCA comes before clustering — not instead of it — is essential.

The reason PCA is used first is computational and statistical. Clustering on 20,000 raw gene dimensions is slow, noisy, and subject to the curse of dimensionality — a phenomenon where high-dimensional spaces are so sparse that distance measures become unreliable. Reducing to 50 PCA components first removes most of the noise while keeping most of the signal, making downstream clustering faster and more robust.

Choosing the Right Task

Before building any model, work through these questions in order. What is my output? A category means classification. A number means regression. Unknown groups means clustering. Do I have labels? Yes means supervised learning — classification or regression. No means unsupervised — clustering. How many output categories? Two means binary classification. More means multi-class classification. And crucially: is a simpler method sufficient? A differential expression test, a t-test, or even PCA visualisation might answer your question without any machine learning at all.

The goal is never to use a particular algorithm. The goal is to answer a biological question. Choose the simplest tool that answers it reliably.

Reality Check
  • Does your biological question actually require machine learning, or would a simpler statistical test suffice?
  • If you found clusters, have you validated them against known biology or just assumed they are real?
  • If you are classifying, where did your labels come from, and could they be wrong?
  • Are you treating a continuous outcome as categorical by binning it? If so, why?
