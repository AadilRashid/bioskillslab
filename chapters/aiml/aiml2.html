Chapter 2: What is a Model?

~35 min Beginner Model, Features, Labels, Parameters, Training, Overfitting

The Biological Question

You have gene expression measurements from 500 tumour samples. Each sample is either lung cancer or breast cancer. Can a computer learn to tell them apart — and then correctly classify a new sample it has never seen before?

This is the core task of machine learning in biology. Before writing a single line of code, you need to understand what a model actually is, what it learns, and why the way you evaluate it matters as much as the algorithm you choose.

A Model is a Function

At its most fundamental level, a model is a function that maps inputs to outputs. You give it measurements, it gives you a prediction.

Gene expression values  →  Model  →  Cancer subtype
DNA sequence            →  Model  →  Regulatory element (yes/no)
Protein sequence        →  Model  →  3D structure
Cell gene expression    →  Model  →  Cell type

The model is not magic. It is a mathematical function — a set of equations with adjustable numbers called parameters or weights. Learning means finding the right values for those parameters so the function produces correct outputs. Before training begins, those numbers are random. After training on thousands of examples, they encode the relationship between your inputs and your outputs.

Features — The Input

Features are the measurements you feed into the model. In genomics, features are almost always numerical: gene expression values (TPM or raw counts for 20,000 genes), presence or absence of a mutation at each genomic position, chromatin accessibility scores across the genome, or protein abundance from proteomics.

Choosing the right features matters enormously. A model can only learn from what you give it. If the signal you care about is not captured in your features, no amount of model complexity will find it. This is one reason why domain knowledge — understanding which biological measurements are relevant — remains essential even in the age of AI.

Labels — The Answer You Are Teaching

In supervised learning, every training example has a label: the correct answer the model is trying to learn to predict. Cancer subtype from pathology reports, tumour vs normal from surgical annotation, patient survival from clinical records, regulatory element from ChIP-seq experiments.

Labels come from you — from experiments, clinical data, or expert annotation. The quality of your labels determines the ceiling of what the model can learn. If two pathologists disagree on cancer subtype for 20% of your training samples, the model cannot reliably exceed roughly 80% accuracy, no matter how sophisticated the algorithm. Garbage labels produce garbage models.

How Learning Actually Happens

The learning algorithm adjusts the model's parameters to make predictions that match the labels. The process is conceptually simple even though the mathematics can be complex:

1. Initialise parameters randomly
2. Feed a training example through the model
3. Compare the prediction to the true label
4. Calculate the error — the "loss"
5. Adjust all parameters slightly to reduce the error
6. Repeat for thousands of examples, thousands of times
7. Stop when predictions are consistently accurate

Step 5 — adjusting parameters based on the error — is called gradient descent. You do not need to understand the calculus. You need to understand that learning equals repeatedly adjusting numbers to reduce prediction error. A deep neural network might run this loop millions of times across billions of training examples. AlphaFold 2 has around 93 million parameters. GPT-4 has hundreds of billions. The process is the same regardless of scale.

What learning does not mean

Learning does not mean the model understands biology. It means the model has found a mathematical function that maps your features to your labels accurately on the training data. A model that predicts cancer subtype with 94% accuracy has learned statistical associations between gene expression patterns and cancer types. It has not discovered why those associations exist. That interpretation is still entirely on you.

Parameters vs Hyperparameters

Two terms that confuse almost every beginner, because they sound similar but refer to completely different things.

Parameters are the numbers inside the model that get adjusted during training. You never set these directly — they are learned from data. When someone says a model has "100 million parameters," they mean it has 100 million adjustable numbers that were all learned from the training data.

Hyperparameters are the settings that control how training works — learning rate (how much to adjust parameters at each step), number of layers in a neural network, number of trees in a random forest, regularisation strength. You set these before training begins. Choosing good hyperparameters is part of the art of machine learning, and part of why simply downloading a model and running it without understanding the settings often produces unreliable results.

Term What it is Who sets it
Parameters Weights inside the model adjusted during training Learned automatically from data
Hyperparameters Settings controlling how training works — learning rate, depth, regularisation Set by you before training

Training vs Testing — The Most Important Distinction

A model that performs perfectly on the data it was trained on is not necessarily useful. It may have simply memorised the training examples rather than learned a generalisable pattern. This is called overfitting — and it is the single most common failure mode in biological AI.

To detect overfitting, you always split your data before training begins:

All data
├── Training set   (~70-80%)  Model learns from this
├── Validation set (~10-15%)  Tune hyperparameters here
└── Test set       (~10-15%)  Final honest evaluation

The test set is held out completely until the very end. You do not use it to make any decisions during training — not for feature selection, not for normalisation, not for hyperparameter tuning. Its performance tells you how well the model will perform on genuinely new, unseen data. That is the only number that matters when deciding whether to trust a model in biology.

Data leakage

If test data accidentally influences training — through preprocessing, feature selection, or normalisation done on the full dataset before splitting — your evaluation is optimistic and your model is not as good as it looks. This happens more often than most published papers acknowledge. We dedicate a full section to it in Chapter 6.

A Model is Not the Truth

The statistician George Box wrote: "All models are wrong, but some are useful." This is especially true in biology.

A model that predicts cancer subtype with 94% accuracy is not a biological truth. It is a useful approximation learned from a specific dataset, a specific feature set, a specific patient population, at a specific point in time. Change any of those and the accuracy may drop dramatically. A model trained on samples from one hospital and tested on samples from another often performs far worse than its original evaluation suggested — not because the biology changed, but because technical and demographic factors differed between cohorts.

Every time you see a model applied to biological data, ask: what data was it trained on? Does my data come from the same distribution? What could it have learned that is not biology — batch effects, cohort biases, technical artifacts?

Reality Check
  • High training accuracy may mean memorisation, not learning
  • Test set performance is the only number that matters
  • A model is only as good as the labels it was trained on
  • Could a simpler statistical test answer the same question?

Running Question

Our running question — can we predict cancer subtype from gene expression — now has concrete meaning. Features: the expression values for each gene. Labels: the cancer subtype from clinical records. Parameters: numbers the model learns from thousands of patients. Test set: the samples we hold out to honestly evaluate whether it works. In Chapter 7 we start building it.
