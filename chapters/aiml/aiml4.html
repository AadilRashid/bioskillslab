Chapter 4: How Do We Know a Model is Good?

~45 min Beginner Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1, AUC, ROC, RMSE, R²

The Biological Question

You have trained a model to classify tumour samples as either cancer or normal. It reports 95% accuracy. Should you trust it?

The answer depends entirely on what 95% actually means — and in biology, accuracy alone is almost never the right metric. This chapter teaches you to ask the right questions about model performance and choose metrics that reflect your biological goals, not just your algorithm's optimism.

Why Accuracy Is Not Enough

Consider a dataset of 1,000 patient samples where 950 are healthy and 50 have a rare cancer. A model that predicts "healthy" for every single patient achieves 95% accuracy. It has also missed every single cancer case.

This is the fundamental problem with accuracy on imbalanced datasets — and genomics datasets are almost always imbalanced. Rare cancers, rare cell types, rare variants. A model that ignores the rare class can look excellent by accuracy while being completely useless clinically.

You need metrics that separately measure how well the model handles each class.

The Confusion Matrix — The Foundation of Everything

Before any metric, you need to understand the confusion matrix. It shows exactly what your model got right and wrong:

                    Predicted
                    Positive    Negative
Actual  Positive:     TP           FN
        Negative:     FP           TN

TP = True Positive   Model said cancer,  patient has cancer    CORRECT
TN = True Negative   Model said normal,  patient is normal     CORRECT
FP = False Positive  Model said cancer,  patient is normal     WRONG
FN = False Negative  Model said normal,  patient has cancer    WRONG

Every metric you will learn derives from these four numbers. Memorise this table — it is the most important 2x2 matrix in machine learning.

Classification Metrics

Accuracy

The proportion of all predictions that were correct:

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)

Useful when classes are balanced. Misleading when they are not — which is most of the time in biology.

Precision — When You Call Something Positive, How Often Are You Right?

Precision answers: of all the samples your model called positive (cancer), what fraction actually were positive?

Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

High precision means low false positive rate. When you call something cancer, you are usually right. This matters when a false positive is costly — for example, when a positive result triggers an aggressive treatment with serious side effects.

Recall (Sensitivity) — Of All the Real Positives, How Many Did You Find?

Recall answers: of all the samples that actually are positive (have cancer), what fraction did your model correctly identify?

Recall = Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)

High recall means low false negative rate. You missed few real cases. This matters when missing a case is costly — for example, when failing to detect cancer means delayed treatment and worse outcomes. In cancer screening, recall is usually more important than precision.

The Precision-Recall Trade-off

Precision and recall are in tension. Increasing one typically decreases the other. If you make your model more conservative — only calling something cancer when very confident — precision goes up but recall goes down. If you make it more liberal — calling anything slightly suspicious cancer — recall goes up but precision falls.

Which matters more depends entirely on the biology and the clinical context. There is no universal answer. This is a decision a bioinformatician must make with domain knowledge, not just optimise algorithmically.

F1 Score — The Harmonic Mean

The F1 score combines precision and recall into a single number:

F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

F1 is high only when both precision and recall are high. It is the standard metric for imbalanced classification problems and is widely used in genomics — DESeq2 benchmarks, variant calling evaluations, and cell type annotation tools all report F1 scores.

Specificity

Specificity is the recall for the negative class — of all the true negatives, how many did you correctly identify?

Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)

In clinical genomics, sensitivity and specificity together describe a test's performance. A cancer screening tool might need sensitivity > 95% (catch almost every case) while maintaining specificity > 90% (not overwhelming clinics with false positives).

ROC Curves and AUC — The Complete Picture

Most classifiers do not directly output a category — they output a probability. "This sample has a 0.73 probability of being cancer." You then apply a threshold: above 0.5 = cancer, below 0.5 = normal.

But why 0.5? The choice of threshold changes your precision and recall. A ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic) curve shows model performance across all possible thresholds simultaneously:

ROC Curve:
  Y-axis: Sensitivity (True Positive Rate)
  X-axis: 1 - Specificity (False Positive Rate)

  Perfect classifier: goes straight up then right (AUC = 1.0)
  Random classifier:  diagonal line from (0,0) to (1,1) (AUC = 0.5)
  Useless classifier: below diagonal (AUC < 0.5)

AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) summarises this into a single number. It represents the probability that the model ranks a random positive sample higher than a random negative sample.

AUC Interpretation:
  0.5   Random guessing — completely useless
  0.7   Acceptable
  0.8   Good
  0.9   Excellent
  1.0   Perfect (almost certainly overfitting)
AUC in single-cell biology

When evaluating cell type classifiers like Geneformer or scGPT, AUC is the standard metric. A paper claiming "our model achieves 99% accuracy" on cell type annotation is far less informative than one reporting per-class F1 scores and AUC values. Always ask which metric was reported and why.

Regression Metrics

When predicting a continuous value (survival time, gene expression level, drug response), classification metrics do not apply. You need regression metrics.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE)

The average absolute difference between predicted and actual values:

MAE = mean(|predicted - actual|)

Easy to interpret. If you are predicting survival time in months and MAE = 4.2, your predictions are off by 4.2 months on average. Units are the same as the prediction.

Mean Squared Error (MSE) and RMSE

MSE  = mean((predicted - actual)²)
RMSE = sqrt(MSE)

MSE penalises large errors more heavily than MAE. RMSE is in the same units as the prediction, making it more interpretable than MSE. Use RMSE when large errors are particularly problematic.

R² — Coefficient of Determination

R² = 1 - (sum of squared residuals / total variance)

R² measures what proportion of the variance in the outcome is explained by the model. R² = 1.0 means perfect prediction. R² = 0 means the model is no better than predicting the mean. R² can be negative if the model is worse than predicting the mean.

In genomics regression tasks — predicting gene expression from chromatin accessibility, for example — R² is the most commonly reported metric.

Which Metric Should You Use?

Situation Recommended metric Why
Balanced classes Accuracy, F1 Classes are roughly equal in size
Imbalanced classes (most biology) F1, AUC, Precision, Recall Accuracy is misleading
Missing a positive case is costly (cancer screening) Recall (Sensitivity) Minimise false negatives
False alarms are costly (rare disease diagnosis) Precision, Specificity Minimise false positives
Comparing classifiers across thresholds AUC-ROC Threshold-independent
Continuous prediction RMSE, R² Classification metrics do not apply
Cell type annotation benchmarks Per-class F1, macro-F1 Many classes, often imbalanced

Cross-Validation — Getting Honest Estimates

A single train/test split gives you one estimate of performance — and that estimate can be lucky or unlucky depending on which samples ended up in which set. Cross-validation gives you a more robust estimate by repeating the process multiple times.

5-fold cross-validation:
  Split data into 5 equal folds
  
  Round 1: Train on folds 2,3,4,5  Test on fold 1
  Round 2: Train on folds 1,3,4,5  Test on fold 2
  Round 3: Train on folds 1,2,4,5  Test on fold 3
  Round 4: Train on folds 1,2,3,5  Test on fold 4
  Round 5: Train on folds 1,2,3,4  Test on fold 5
  
  Final performance = mean across all 5 test sets

Cross-validation is the standard for small to medium datasets. For very large datasets a single held-out test set is usually sufficient. In genomics, where sample sizes are often small (tens to hundreds of patients), cross-validation is almost always the right choice.

The metric you choose is the model you get

When you optimise a model to maximise a metric, you get a model that is good at that metric — not necessarily at the biological task. If you optimise for accuracy on an imbalanced dataset, you get a model that ignores the rare class. Always choose metrics that reflect what matters biologically, then optimise for those.

A Note on p-values vs AUC

Many biologists are more comfortable with p-values than with AUC or F1 scores. They are measuring different things:

  • A p-value tells you whether a difference is likely due to chance
  • An AUC tells you how well you can predict which class a sample belongs to
  • An effect size tells you how large the difference is

All three are necessary. A gene can be statistically significant (low p-value), have a large effect size (high fold change), but still be a poor classifier (low AUC) if the expression distributions overlap too much. The Statistics for Biology course covered the first two. This course adds the third.

Reality Check
  • What metric was used to evaluate this model? Is it appropriate for the class balance?
  • Was performance reported on a truly held-out test set or on training/validation data?
  • Does high AUC on the training cohort hold up on an independent validation cohort?
  • Could a simpler baseline — predicting the majority class — achieve similar accuracy?
