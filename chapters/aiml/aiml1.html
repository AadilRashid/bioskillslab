~20 min Beginner AI, ML, Deep Learning, LLMs, Foundation Models

Two Things That Are Both True

AI has predicted protein structures that would have taken years of experimental work to determine. AlphaFold2 solved a problem structural biologists had worked on for half a century, in months.

AI has also "discovered" biological patterns that later turned out to be technical artifacts, batch effects, or data leakage. Papers have been published. Some have been retracted.

The one skill this course teaches

Understanding the difference between those two outcomes is the most important skill in modern computational biology. Everything in this course builds toward that judgement.

Why Biology Needs AI Now

The data is no longer the bottleneck. Making sense of it is.

  • A single RNA-seq run: 20,000 gene measurements per sample
  • A single-cell experiment: millions of individual cells profiled
  • A whole genome: 3 billion base pairs per patient

Human intuition and t-tests were not designed for this scale. AI was.

What Each Term Means

Artificial Intelligence

The broad goal: make computers perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — image recognition, language, decision-making, pattern discovery.

Machine Learning

Instead of writing rules manually, show the model many examples and let it learn the relationship. Gene expression in, cancer subtype out.

Deep Learning

Neural networks that learn their own features from raw data. Instead of you selecting 100 important genes, the network learns what matters from all 20,000.

Transformers

Neural networks designed for sequences. They can look at any part of a sequence when interpreting any other part — critical for DNA where regulatory elements can be 500,000 bp away.

LLMs

Transformers trained on enormous datasets. The same architecture trained on DNA sequences becomes a DNA language model. Trained on proteins, a protein language model.

Foundation Models

Large models pretrained on enormous biological datasets that can be adapted to many tasks. They are the frontier of modern computational biology. We cover them in Chapters 12-15.
