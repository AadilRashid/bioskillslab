Unsupervised Learning: Finding Structure Without Labels

In supervised learning (classification, regression), you have labels. You know which samples are cancer and which are normal. In unsupervised learning, you don't have labels. You're asking: what structure exists in this data? Do samples naturally group together? Are there patterns in gene expression across thousands of genes?

This is the dominant mode of analysis in genomics. Most of the time, you don't know the answer before you look.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

You have 20,000 genes measured across 50 samples. That's a 50 × 20,000 matrix. You can't visualize it. PCA compresses it into 2-3 dimensions while preserving as much variation as possible.

How it works

PCA finds new axes (principal components) that capture the most variance in the data. PC1 captures the most variance, PC2 the second most, and so on. Each PC is a linear combination of the original variables.

# In R with DESeq2:
vsd <- vst(dds)                    # Variance-stabilizing transform
plotPCA(vsd, intgroup = "condition")

# Or manually:
pca <- prcomp(t(assay(vsd)), scale = TRUE)
summary(pca)$importance[, 1:5]    # Variance explained by each PC

# PC1 explains 45% of variance
# PC2 explains 18% of variance
# Together: 63% of total variance in 2 dimensions

What to look for in a PCA plot

  • Samples cluster by condition: treated and control separate along PC1. Good sign.
  • Samples cluster by batch: batch 1 and batch 2 separate. Problem. Correct for batch.
  • Outlier samples: one sample far from all others. Investigate. May need to remove.
  • No separation: conditions overlap completely. The treatment may not affect global expression.
PCA is not clustering

PCA shows you the structure of the data. It doesn't assign samples to groups. If you see two clusters in a PCA plot, you still need a clustering algorithm to formally assign samples to those clusters.

k-means Clustering

Partition samples into k groups by minimizing within-cluster variance. You specify k in advance.

# In R:
set.seed(42)
km <- kmeans(t(assay(vsd)), centers = 3, nstart = 25)
km$cluster    # Which cluster each sample belongs to

# How to choose k? Elbow plot:
wss <- sapply(1:10, function(k) {
  kmeans(data, centers = k, nstart = 25)$tot.withinss
})
plot(1:10, wss, type = "b", xlab = "k", ylab = "Within-cluster SS")

Limitations of k-means:

  • You must specify k in advance
  • Assumes spherical clusters of similar size
  • Sensitive to outliers
  • Results vary with random initialization (use nstart > 1)

Hierarchical Clustering

Builds a tree (dendrogram) of samples based on pairwise distances. No need to specify k in advance. You cut the tree at a height to get clusters.

# In R:
dist_mat <- dist(t(assay(vsd)))          # Euclidean distance
hc <- hclust(dist_mat, method = "ward.D2")
plot(hc)                                  # Dendrogram

# Cut into 3 clusters:
clusters <- cutree(hc, k = 3)

# Heatmap with hierarchical clustering:
pheatmap(assay(vsd)[top_genes, ],
         clustering_method = "ward.D2",
         annotation_col = coldata)

Distance metrics:

Euclidean:    Straight-line distance. Sensitive to scale.
Correlation:  1 - Pearson r. Good for gene expression (ignores magnitude).
Manhattan:    Sum of absolute differences. Robust to outliers.

t-SNE and UMAP: Nonlinear Dimensionality Reduction

PCA is linear. It can only capture linear structure. t-SNE and UMAP can capture complex nonlinear structure, which is why they're the standard for single-cell RNA-Seq visualization.

UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection)

# In R (Seurat for single-cell):
seurat_obj <- RunPCA(seurat_obj)
seurat_obj <- RunUMAP(seurat_obj, dims = 1:30)
DimPlot(seurat_obj, reduction = "umap", group.by = "cell_type")

Key differences from PCA:

  • Preserves local structure (nearby cells stay nearby)
  • Global distances are NOT meaningful (clusters far apart on UMAP may not be biologically distant)
  • Results depend on hyperparameters (n_neighbors, min_dist)
  • Not deterministic — different runs give different layouts
UMAP misinterpretation

The distance between clusters on a UMAP plot is not biologically meaningful. Two clusters that look far apart might be transcriptionally similar. Two clusters that look close might be very different. UMAP is for visualization only. Use actual gene expression distances for biological comparisons.

Clustering in Bioinformatics

  • Sample QC: PCA to detect outliers and batch effects before DE analysis
  • Gene co-expression modules: WGCNA clusters genes with similar expression patterns
  • Single-cell cell type discovery: Leiden/Louvain clustering on UMAP
  • Patient stratification: Cluster patients by molecular profile to find subtypes
  • Heatmap organization: Hierarchical clustering orders genes and samples in heatmaps
