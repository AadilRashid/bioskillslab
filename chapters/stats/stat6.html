Chapter 6: Statistical Significance is Not Biological Significance

~35 min Beginner Effect Size, Power, Interpretation

The Two Meanings of "Significant"

In everyday English, "significant" means "important." In statistics, it means "unlikely to be due to chance." These are not the same thing, and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in science.

A result can be statistically significant but biologically trivial. A result can be statistically non-significant but biologically important. You need to think about both.

When a Significant Result Doesn't Matter

You run RNA-Seq on 100 samples per group. DESeq2 finds 5,000 genes with padj < 0.05. Exciting? Look closer. Most of them have log2 fold changes between -0.2 and 0.2. That's a 15% change in expression. Statistically real? Yes. Biologically meaningful? Almost certainly not. Cells have noise. A 15% fluctuation in a housekeeping gene isn't going to change anyone's biology.

Why this happens

With large sample sizes, you have enormous statistical power. You can detect tiny effects with high confidence. But "detectable" and "meaningful" are different things. The p-value tells you the effect is real. It doesn't tell you the effect matters.

When a Non-Significant Result Does Matter

You compare a new drug to placebo in 8 patients. The drug group improves by 40%, but p = 0.12. "Not significant." Does that mean the drug doesn't work?

No. It means you didn't have enough patients to detect the effect. With n=8, you have very low statistical power. The effect might be real and large, but your sample was too small to confirm it. A non-significant result is not evidence of no effect. It's evidence of insufficient data.

Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence

"We found no significant difference" does NOT mean "there is no difference." It means "we couldn't detect a difference with this sample size." Always check the confidence interval. If it's wide enough to include both "no effect" and "large effect," you simply don't know.

Effect Size: What You Should Actually Report

Effect size measures how big the difference is, independent of sample size. Common effect sizes in bioinformatics:

Differential expression:  log2 fold change
Correlation:              Pearson r or Spearman ρ
Group comparison:         Cohen's d = (mean₁ - mean₂) / pooled SD
Variant association:      Odds ratio
Explained variance:       R²

Rules of thumb for log2 fold change

|log2FC| < 0.5    Small effect (< 1.4-fold change)
|log2FC| 0.5-1.0  Moderate effect (1.4 to 2-fold)
|log2FC| > 1.0    Large effect (> 2-fold)
|log2FC| > 2.0    Very large effect (> 4-fold)

Most DE analyses use |log2FC| > 1 as a biological significance cutoff alongside padj < 0.05 as a statistical significance cutoff. You need both.

The Volcano Plot: Seeing Both at Once

A volcano plot puts effect size (log2FC) on the x-axis and statistical significance (-log10 padj) on the y-axis. The interesting genes are in the upper corners: large effect AND statistically significant.

        -log10(padj)
            │
     ★      │      ★       ← Significant AND large effect
            │
     •      │      •       ← Significant but small effect
   ─────────┼──────────── padj = 0.05
     •      │      •       ← Not significant
            │
   ─────────┼────────────
        -log2FC    +log2FC
            │
       ← down    up →

The dots in the middle top (small fold change, low p-value) are statistically significant but biologically boring. The dots in the far left/right but below the line are biologically interesting but not confirmed statistically. Only the upper corners give you both.

Practical Guidelines

  • Always report effect size alongside p-values
  • Use both statistical (padj < 0.05) and biological (|log2FC| > 1) cutoffs for DE
  • Don't say "no effect" when you mean "not significant." Say "we did not detect a significant effect"
  • Look at confidence intervals. A wide CI means you don't have enough data to draw conclusions
  • A significant result with a tiny effect size is usually not worth following up on
  • A non-significant result with a large effect size and wide CI needs more replicates, not a different conclusion
