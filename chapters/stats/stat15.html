Chapter 15: Statistical Traps to Avoid

~40 min Intermediate P-hacking, HARKing, Reproducibility, Common Mistakes

Why Good Scientists Get Bad Results

Most researchers who publish false results aren't fraudsters. They're well-intentioned scientists who fell into statistical traps that are easy to fall into and hard to notice. This chapter is about recognizing those traps before you step in them.

Trap 1: P-Hacking

Running multiple analyses and only reporting the one that gives p < 0.05. This inflates your false positive rate without any formal correction.

Forms of p-hacking:
- Trying different subgroup splits until one is significant
- Adding or removing covariates until p < 0.05
- Collecting data until p < 0.05, then stopping
- Trying multiple statistical tests and reporting the best one
- Removing "outliers" that hurt your p-value
- Rounding p = 0.054 to p = 0.05

The fix: pre-register your analysis plan. Decide your primary outcome, sample size, and statistical test before collecting data. Stick to it.

Trap 2: HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known)

You run an exploratory analysis, find an interesting pattern, then write the paper as if you predicted that pattern all along. The hypothesis was generated by the data, not before it.

This is a problem because exploratory findings need to be validated in independent data. If you present them as confirmatory, readers think they're more reliable than they are.

Honest reporting:
"We explored gene expression patterns and found that GENE_X
was upregulated. This is a hypothesis-generating finding that
requires validation in an independent cohort."

HARKing:
"We hypothesized that GENE_X would be upregulated based on
its known role in [pathway]. We confirmed this hypothesis."
(But you only looked at GENE_X because it came up in your analysis)

Trap 3: Ignoring Multiple Testing

Already covered in Chapter 7, but worth repeating: using raw p-values on genome-wide data is wrong. Always use adjusted p-values. Always.

Trap 4: Confusing Statistical and Biological Significance

Already covered in Chapter 6. A tiny effect can be statistically significant with enough samples. Always report effect sizes.

Trap 5: Regression to the Mean

If you select samples based on an extreme measurement, their next measurement will tend to be less extreme. This is regression to the mean, and it can look like a treatment effect.

Example: You select the 10 patients with the highest tumor expression.
You treat them and measure again. Expression is lower.
Did the treatment work? Maybe. Or maybe they just regressed to the mean.

Fix: Always include a control group that was selected the same way
but received no treatment.

Trap 6: Survivorship Bias

You only analyze samples that made it through your pipeline. Samples that failed QC, patients who dropped out, genes with too few reads — these are not random. Excluding them can bias your results.

Example: You exclude samples with low mapping rates.
But low mapping rates might be associated with sample quality,
which might be associated with disease severity.
Your remaining samples are not representative of the original population.

Trap 7: Circular Analysis

Using the same data to both select features and evaluate performance. Common in machine learning.

Wrong:
1. Select top 100 genes based on differential expression in all 50 samples
2. Train a classifier on those 100 genes using all 50 samples
3. Report 95% accuracy

The classifier was trained on the same data used to select features.
The accuracy is wildly optimistic.

Right:
1. Split data into training and test sets FIRST
2. Select features using only training data
3. Train classifier on training data
4. Evaluate on test data (which was never touched during steps 1-2)

Trap 8: Ignoring Assumptions

Every statistical test has assumptions. Violating them gives wrong p-values.

t-test assumes:     Gaussian data, equal variances
ANOVA assumes:      Gaussian data, equal variances, independence
Pearson r assumes:  Linear relationship, no outliers
Chi-squared assumes: Expected counts > 5 in each cell

Always check your assumptions before interpreting results.

Trap 9: The Winner's Curse

The first study to report an association tends to overestimate the effect size. This is because small studies only reach significance when they happen to get an unusually large effect by chance.

True effect: log2FC = 1.5
Study 1 (n=5): reports log2FC = 3.2, p = 0.04 (lucky sample)
Study 2 (n=50): reports log2FC = 1.6, p = 0.001 (accurate)

The first study's effect size is inflated. Replication studies
with larger samples typically show smaller effects.

Building Reproducible Analyses

1. Pre-register your analysis plan
2. Use version control (Git) for all code
3. Document every parameter and software version
4. Report all results, not just significant ones
5. Make code and data available
6. Validate findings in independent datasets
7. Use appropriate multiple testing correction
8. Report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values
9. Distinguish exploratory from confirmatory analyses
10. Have someone else reproduce your analysis from your code
The one habit that prevents most traps

Write down your analysis plan before you look at the data. What is your primary outcome? What test will you use? What cutoffs? What will you do with outliers? Committing to these decisions in advance prevents most forms of p-hacking and HARKing.
