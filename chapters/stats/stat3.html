Chapter 3: Distributions — The Shape of Your Data

~40 min Beginner Gaussian, Lognormal, Poisson, Skewness

Why Distributions Matter

Before you run any statistical test, you need to know what shape your data takes. Most tests assume a specific distribution. Use the wrong one and your p-values, confidence intervals, and conclusions are all garbage.

The good news: biological data tends to follow a handful of well-understood distributions. Learn these and you'll know which test to reach for in almost any situation.

The Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

The bell curve. Symmetric, centered on the mean, with most values clustered in the middle and fewer values in the tails.

68% of values fall within 1 SD of the mean
95% of values fall within 2 SD of the mean
99.7% of values fall within 3 SD of the mean

Where you see it in bioinformatics:

  • Log2 fold changes from differential expression (roughly Gaussian)
  • Quality scores across a sequencing run
  • Measurement errors in quantitative assays

Key properties:

  • Defined by two numbers: mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ)
  • Symmetric around the mean
  • Tails extend to infinity but values far from the mean are rare

The Lognormal Distribution

Take a Gaussian distribution and exponentiate it. You get a lognormal: right-skewed, always positive, with a long tail of high values.

0        median      → long tail

Where you see it in bioinformatics:

  • Gene expression counts. Most genes have low expression. A few are highly expressed. This is the classic lognormal shape.
  • Protein abundance across a proteome
  • Genome sizes across species
  • Read depths across genomic positions
Practical tip

If your data is right-skewed (long tail to the right), try taking the log. If the logged data looks Gaussian, your original data is lognormal. This is why we use log2 fold changes and log-transformed expression values everywhere in genomics.

The Poisson Distribution

Counts of rare events in a fixed interval. How many reads map to a given position? How many mutations in a gene? How many cells express a marker?

For λ = 3 (average 3 events):

P(0) = ▌         5%
P(1) = ███       15%
P(2) = █████     22%
P(3) = █████     22%
P(4) = ████      17%
P(5) = ██        10%
P(6) = █          5%
P(7) = ▌          2%

Key properties:

  • Defined by one number: λ (the average rate)
  • Mean equals variance (if mean = 5, variance ≈ 5)
  • Only produces non-negative integers (0, 1, 2, 3...)
  • Approaches Gaussian as λ gets large

Where you see it in bioinformatics:

  • Read counts at a genomic position (idealized)
  • Number of mutations per gene
  • Number of reads in a ChIP-Seq peak

The Negative Binomial Distribution

In reality, RNA-Seq count data has more variability than Poisson predicts. The variance is larger than the mean. This is called overdispersion, and it's why DESeq2 and edgeR use the negative binomial distribution instead of Poisson.

Poisson:            variance = mean
Negative binomial:  variance = mean + α × mean²

α (dispersion) captures the extra biological variability
between replicates that Poisson can't account for.
This is why Poisson is wrong for RNA-Seq

If you model RNA-Seq counts as Poisson, you underestimate the variance, which inflates your test statistics and gives you too many false positives. DESeq2 exists specifically to handle this overdispersion correctly.

The Binomial Distribution

How many successes in a fixed number of trials? Flip a coin 10 times, how many heads? Sequence 100 reads at a heterozygous SNP, how many show the alternate allele?

Where you see it:

  • Allele frequencies at a variant site
  • Proportion of reads supporting a variant (variant allele frequency)
  • Proportion of cells expressing a gene (single-cell)

How to Tell Which Distribution Your Data Follows

1. Plot a histogram. What shape do you see?
   - Symmetric bell → probably Gaussian
   - Right-skewed → try log transform, might be lognormal
   - Counts of things → Poisson or negative binomial

2. Q-Q plot: plot your data quantiles against theoretical quantiles
   - Points on the diagonal line → data matches that distribution
   - Curved away → doesn't match

3. In R:
   hist(x)                    # histogram
   qqnorm(x); qqline(x)      # Q-Q plot against Gaussian
   shapiro.test(x)            # formal normality test (small samples only)
