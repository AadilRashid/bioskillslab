Chapter 4: Confidence Intervals — What They Actually Mean

~40 min Beginner CI, Standard Error, Precision, Uncertainty

The Problem with Point Estimates

You measure the average expression of BRCA1 across 5 tumor samples and get 847 counts. Is the true average 847? Almost certainly not. It could be 750 or 950 or anything in between. A single number (point estimate) tells you nothing about how precise that estimate is.

A confidence interval fixes this. Instead of saying "the mean is 847," you say "the mean is somewhere between 720 and 974." That range tells you how much uncertainty you're dealing with.

Standard Deviation vs Standard Error

These two get confused constantly. They measure completely different things.

Standard Deviation (SD)

How spread out are the individual values? If you measure the weight of 10 mice and get SD = 3g, that means individual mice vary by about 3g from the average. SD describes the population's variability.

Standard Error (SE)

How precise is your estimate of the mean? SE tells you how much the sample mean would bounce around if you repeated the experiment. SE describes the precision of your estimate.

SE = SD / √n

SD = 3g, n = 10 mice:  SE = 3/√10 = 0.95g
SD = 3g, n = 100 mice: SE = 3/√100 = 0.30g

Notice: SD stays the same (mice are still variable), but SE shrinks as you add more mice. More data = more precise estimate.

Common mistake in papers

Bar plots with "error bars" that don't say whether they show SD or SE. SD bars are wider (showing variability). SE bars are narrower (showing precision). A paper showing SE bars on n=3 makes the data look much more precise than it actually is. Always check the figure legend.

What a 95% Confidence Interval Means

A 95% CI does NOT mean "there's a 95% chance the true value is in this range." The true value is fixed. It's either in the range or it isn't.

What it actually means: if you repeated this experiment 100 times and calculated a CI each time, about 95 of those intervals would contain the true value. It's a statement about the procedure, not about any single interval.

For a mean (approximately):

95% CI ≈ sample mean ± 2 × SE

Example:
  Mean expression = 847
  SD = 120, n = 5
  SE = 120/√5 = 53.7
  95% CI ≈ 847 ± 107 = [740, 954]

That range is wide because n=5 is small. With n=50:

  SE = 120/√50 = 17.0
  95% CI ≈ 847 ± 34 = [813, 881]

Ten times more samples, three times narrower interval. That's the power of replication.

CIs are More Useful Than P-Values

A p-value tells you "significant or not." Binary. A confidence interval tells you the range of plausible values for the effect. Much more informative.

Study A: log2FC = 2.1, p = 0.03, 95% CI = [0.2, 4.0]
Study B: log2FC = 2.1, p = 0.03, 95% CI = [1.8, 2.4]

Same fold change, same p-value. But Study A's CI is enormous (the true effect could be anywhere from tiny to huge). Study B's CI is tight (the effect is precisely estimated around 2). The CI tells you which result to trust.

CIs in Bioinformatics

Differential expression

DESeq2 reports log2FoldChange and lfcSE (log fold change standard error). You can compute the CI:

# In R, after running DESeq2:
res$CI_lower <- res$log2FoldChange - 1.96 * res$lfcSE
res$CI_upper <- res$log2FoldChange + 1.96 * res$lfcSE

# A gene with log2FC = 3.0 and lfcSE = 0.5:
# CI = [2.02, 3.98] — real effect, reasonably precise

# A gene with log2FC = 3.0 and lfcSE = 2.0:
# CI = [-0.92, 6.92] — could be anything, including no effect

Variant allele frequency

You see 15 reads with the alternate allele out of 30 total. VAF = 50%. But the 95% CI is [31%, 69%]. With only 30 reads, you can't pin down the frequency precisely.

Proportion of cells expressing a gene (single-cell)

12 out of 50 cells express Gene X. That's 24%. But the 95% CI is [13%, 38%]. Report the interval, not just the point estimate.

How to Make CIs Narrower

  • Increase sample size. SE = SD/√n. More n = smaller SE = narrower CI.
  • Reduce variability. Better experimental control, matched designs, blocking by batch.
  • Accept less confidence. A 90% CI is narrower than 95%. But you're less confident.
Rule of thumb

To halve the width of a confidence interval, you need 4 times the sample size. Going from n=3 to n=12 cuts the CI width in half. This is why the jump from 2 to 3 replicates matters so much, but going from 10 to 11 barely helps.
