Chapter 1: Why Statistics? Your Data is Lying to You

~30 min Beginner Intuition, Bias, Probability, Variability

Your Brain is Bad at Statistics

Before learning any formulas, you need to understand something uncomfortable: human intuition about data is reliably wrong. We see patterns in noise, we jump to conclusions from small samples, and we confuse correlation with causation every single day.

This isn't a character flaw. It's how brains evolved. Spotting a tiger in the grass once and assuming all rustling grass means tigers kept our ancestors alive. But in a genomics lab, that same instinct makes you see "significant" results that aren't real.

We See Patterns in Random Data

Run a differential expression analysis on two groups of completely random data. No real biological difference. You'll still find "significant" genes. With 20,000 genes tested at p < 0.05, you expect 1,000 false positives by pure chance. That's not a bug in the software. That's how probability works.

We Jump to Conclusions from Small Samples

A colleague runs an experiment with 2 replicates per condition and finds a 3-fold change in their favorite gene. Exciting? Maybe. But with n=2, the confidence interval around that fold change is enormous. It could easily be 0.5-fold or 10-fold. You simply can't tell with that little data.

This is the single most common mistake in bioinformatics: drawing strong conclusions from underpowered experiments.

We Confuse Correlation with Causation

Gene A and Gene B are co-expressed across 50 samples. Does A regulate B? Does B regulate A? Are they both regulated by some third factor C? Co-expression tells you nothing about causation. It only tells you the two things move together.

We are Overconfident

Ask a researcher how confident they are in their results. Most will say "very." Ask them to put a number on it. They'll say 95%. But when you actually look at reproducibility studies, about 50-70% of published findings fail to replicate. Our confidence far exceeds our accuracy.

What Statistics Actually Does

Statistics is a set of tools for making decisions under uncertainty. You never have perfect data. You always have noise, variability, and limited samples. Statistics helps you answer one question: is the signal I'm seeing real, or could it be explained by chance?

The Core Problem

You measure gene expression in 3 treated and 3 control samples. The treated group has higher expression. But is the difference real, or did you just happen to pick 3 high-expression samples by luck?

That's it. That's the fundamental question behind every statistical test, every p-value, every confidence interval. The rest is just machinery for answering it rigorously.

Variability is the Enemy

If every mouse responded identically to a drug, you'd only need one mouse per group. But they don't. Biological variability means every measurement is a mix of signal (the real effect) and noise (random variation between individuals, technical variation, batch effects).

The job of statistics is to separate signal from noise. More replicates help because they average out the noise. Better experimental design helps because it reduces the noise in the first place.

Sources of Variability in Genomics

  • Biological variation between individuals (genetics, age, sex, environment)
  • Technical variation in library prep, sequencing depth, batch effects
  • Computational variation from alignment parameters, counting methods, normalization

A good bioinformatician understands all three and designs analyses to account for them.

Why Bioinformaticians Need Statistics

Every major bioinformatics task involves statistics:

  • Differential expression uses negative binomial models and FDR correction
  • Variant calling uses Bayesian probability to distinguish real variants from sequencing errors
  • Clustering (single-cell, patient subtypes) uses distance metrics and dimensionality reduction
  • GWAS tests millions of variants and needs extreme multiple testing correction
  • Survival analysis compares patient outcomes between groups

You don't need to derive the math. But you need to understand what each method assumes, when it breaks, and how to interpret the output. That's what this course teaches.

The one thing to remember from this chapter

Your intuition about data is unreliable. Statistics exists because we need a disciplined way to ask "is this real?" Every time you look at a result and think "that looks significant," stop and ask: what would I expect to see by chance alone?
