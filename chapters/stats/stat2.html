Chapter 2: From Sample to Population

~35 min Beginner Sampling, Bias, Parameters, Inference

The Big Idea

You can never measure every member of a population. You sequence 6 tumor samples, not every tumor on earth. You measure gene expression in 4 mice, not every mouse of that strain. Statistics lets you take what you learned from your small sample and make reasonable statements about the larger population it came from.

This leap from sample to population is called statistical inference, and it's the entire point of statistics.

Population vs Sample

The population is the complete group you care about. The sample is the subset you actually measured.

Population: All humans with BRCA1 mutations
Sample:     The 200 patients in your study

Population: All possible RNA-Seq measurements of HeLa cells treated with Drug X
Sample:     Your 3 treated replicates

Population: All E. coli in a patient's gut
Sample:     The colonies you picked and sequenced

The population is often abstract or infinite. You'll never sequence every E. coli in someone's gut. But you want to say something about the population based on what you saw in your sample.

Parameters vs Statistics

A parameter is a number that describes the population. A statistic is a number calculated from your sample that estimates that parameter.

Parameter (population)     Statistic (sample)
─────────────────────      ──────────────────
True mean expression       Sample mean (x̄)
True proportion mutated    Sample proportion (p̂)
True correlation           Sample correlation (r)

You never know the true parameter. You only have the sample statistic. The gap between them is called sampling error, and it's unavoidable.

Sampling Error

Take 3 mice from a colony and measure their weight. The average is 22g. Take a different 3 mice from the same colony. The average might be 24g. Neither is wrong. Both are valid samples. They just happened to grab different mice.

This variation between samples is sampling error. It's not a mistake. It's a mathematical consequence of measuring a subset instead of the whole.

Key insight

Sampling error gets smaller as your sample gets larger. With 3 mice, the sample mean bounces around a lot. With 30 mice, it's much more stable. With 300, it barely moves. This is why sample size matters so much.

Sampling Bias

Sampling error is random and unavoidable. Sampling bias is systematic and avoidable. Bias means your sample doesn't represent the population.

Examples in bioinformatics:

  • Sequencing only tumors from one hospital (geographic bias)
  • Running all treated samples in batch 1 and all controls in batch 2 (confounded batch effect)
  • Excluding samples that "look weird" before analysis (selection bias)
  • Using only cell lines instead of primary tissue (model bias)

No amount of statistical analysis fixes a biased sample. If your treated and control samples were processed on different days, you can't tell whether the difference you see is from the treatment or from the batch. This is why experimental design matters more than any statistical test.

Random Sampling

The ideal is random sampling: every member of the population has an equal chance of being in your sample. In practice, this is almost never perfectly achieved in biology. You use the mice available in your colony, the patients who happen to visit your hospital, the cell lines in your freezer.

The question to always ask: is my sample representative of the population I want to generalize to? If you study gene expression in HeLa cells, your results apply to HeLa cells. Not to "human cells" in general. Not to cervical cancer in general. Just HeLa.

Multiple Levels of Sampling

In genomics, sampling happens at multiple levels:

Level 1: Which patients/animals you select     (biological sampling)
Level 2: Which cells you extract RNA from       (tissue sampling)
Level 3: Which RNA molecules get sequenced       (library sampling)
Level 4: Which reads pass quality filters        (computational sampling)

Variability enters at every level. A good experiment controls for as many levels as possible through randomization, replication, and careful protocol.

What This Means for Your Analysis

  • Your sample mean is an estimate of the true mean, not the true mean itself
  • Every estimate has uncertainty, which we quantify with confidence intervals
  • Bigger samples give more precise estimates (less sampling error)
  • Biased samples give wrong estimates no matter how large they are
  • Statistical tests ask: "given the sampling error I expect, is this result surprising?"
